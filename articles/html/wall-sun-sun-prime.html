In number theory, a Wall–Sun–Sun prime or Fibonacci–Wieferich prime is a certain kind of prime number which is conjectured to exist, although none are known.

Definition

Let p {\displaystyle p} be a prime number. When each term in the sequence of Fibonacci numbers F n {\displaystyle F_{n}} is reduced modulo p {\displaystyle p} , the result is a periodic sequence. The (minimal) period length of this sequence is called the Pisano period and denoted π ( p ) {\displaystyle \pi (p)} . Since F 0 = 0 {\displaystyle F_{0}=0} , it follows that p divides F π ( p ) {\displaystyle F_{\pi (p)}} . A prime p such that p2 divides F π ( p ) {\displaystyle F_{\pi (p)}} is called a Wall–Sun–Sun prime.

Existence

Unsolved problem in mathematics:

Are there any Wall–Sun–Sun primes? If yes, are there an infinite number of them?

(more unsolved problems in mathematics)

In a study of the Pisano period k ( p ) {\displaystyle k(p)} , Donald Dines Wall determined that there are no Wall–Sun–Sun primes less than 10000 {\displaystyle 10000} . In 1960, he wrote:

The most perplexing problem we have met in this study concerns the hypothesis k ( p 2 ) ≠ k ( p ) {\displaystyle k(p^{2})\neq k(p)} . We have run a test on digital computer which shows that k ( p 2 ) ≠ k ( p ) {\displaystyle k(p^{2})\neq k(p)} for all p {\displaystyle p} up to 10000 {\displaystyle 10000} ; however, we cannot prove that k ( p 2 ) = k ( p ) {\displaystyle k(p^{2})=k(p)} is impossible. The question is closely related to another one, "can a number x {\displaystyle x} have the same order mod p {\displaystyle p} and mod p 2 {\displaystyle p^{2}} ?", for which rare cases give an affirmative answer (e.g., x = 3 , p = 11 {\displaystyle x=3,p=11} ; x = 2 , p = 1093 {\displaystyle x=2,p=1093} ); hence, one might conjecture that equality may hold for some exceptional p {\displaystyle p} .

It has since been conjectured that there are infinitely many Wall–Sun–Sun primes. No Wall–Sun–Sun primes are known as of December 2020 [update] .

In 2007, Richard J. McIntosh and Eric L. Roettger showed that if any exist, they must be > 2×1014. Dorais and Klyve extended this range to 9.7×1014 without finding such a prime.

In December 2011, another search was started by the PrimeGrid project, however it was suspended in May 2017. In November 2020, PrimeGrid started another project that searches for Wieferich and Wall–Sun–Sun primes simultaneously. As of December 2020 [update] , its leading edge is over 300 ⋅ 10 15 {\displaystyle 300\cdot 10^{15}} .

History

Wall–Sun–Sun primes are named after Donald Dines Wall, Zhi Hong Sun and Zhi Wei Sun; Z. H. Sun and Z. W. Sun showed in 1992 that if the first case of Fermat's Last Theorem was false for a certain prime p, then p would have to be a Wall–Sun–Sun prime. As a result, prior to Andrew Wiles' proof of Fermat's Last Theorem, the search for Wall–Sun–Sun primes was also the search for a potential counterexample to this centuries-old conjecture.

Generalizations

A tribonacci–Wieferich prime is a prime p satisfying h(p) = h(p2), where h is the least positive integer satisfying [Th,Th+1,Th+2] ≡ [T0, T1, T2] (mod m) and Tn denotes the n-th tribonacci number. No tribonacci–Wieferich prime exists below 1011.

A Pell–Wieferich prime is a prime p satisfying p2 divides Pp−1, when p congruent to 1 or 7 (mod 8), or p2 divides Pp+1, when p congruent to 3 or 5 (mod 8), where Pn denotes the n-th Pell number. For example, 13, 31, and 1546463 are Pell–Wieferich primes, and no others below 109 (sequence A238736 in the OEIS). In fact, Pell–Wieferich primes are 2-Wall–Sun–Sun primes.

Near-Wall–Sun–Sun primes

A prime p such that F p − ( p 5 ) ≡ A p ( mod p 2 ) {\displaystyle F_{p-\left({\frac {p}{5}}\right)}\equiv Ap{\pmod {p^{2}}}} with small |A| is called near-Wall–Sun–Sun prime. Near-Wall–Sun–Sun primes with A = 0 would be Wall–Sun–Sun primes. PrimeGrid records cases with |A| ≤ 1000. A dozen cases is known where A = ±1 (sequence A347565 in the OEIS).
